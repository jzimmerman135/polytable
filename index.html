Introduction, Inspiration and Design Philosophy

In Notes on the Synthesis of Form, architect Christopher Alexander suggests that we must design forms that fit their context. According to Alexander, good fit is extremely hard to invent or describe, but that by identifying misfits between the form and context, we can create natural designs. This proposal intends to systematically resolve misfits between today's cultural context and forms of social media, resulting in a distributed media platform whose form enriches it's context.

Another crucial resource was Joanne K. Cheung's reflection User's vs Muses, which paints the 21st century as the "Century of the User", and questions the dynamic that users play when we engage with mainstream technologies today. Her reflections on how designers can empower users, were highly influential.

Finally, this paper draws on the vision of Doug Engelbart â€” whose mission was to augment human intelligence. For Doug in 1968, it was the desire to augment human intelligence that lead him to invent the user interfaces and many computing features we would still consider advanced today. In this paper we update Doug's questioning to ask, can technology be used to augment a culture?.

When reading the rest of this paper, it is important to remember that we often think of a social media as successful when it is
  • Visited many times per day
  • Fun and digestibile enough that we use it for hours on end
  • Socially and politically powerful
  • Embedded in and altering our social fabric
  • Making its parent company wealthy beyond comprehension
I'd suggest though, that these qualities actually identify an un-successful social media. In many respects this project seeks to intentionally oppose these values, so I ask that you try to resist skeptecism based on these metrics.

Dynamics of Engagement

When we call an application design as a "social media", we could be referring to any number of forms. However, the primary thread between them being is always idea that the "users" (human or corporate) create the content. Theoretically this means that the creators and the consumers are the same people, but for the most popular social media platforms that this is hardly true. The most popular social medias operate more as an entertainment platform than a virtual community space. When we view these platforms as a stage, the dynamics of how we engage with social media, are as performers or witnesses.

Platforms vary greatly in the kinds of performances they encourage, long performances like Youtube, short performances like TikTok, social performance like Instagram. Even platforms like Twitter where there theoretically isn't a difference in the mechanisms performance and witnessing, the platform still encourages a dynamic where few popular users will perform clever quips to be witnessed by the majority of other users. As long as there is a concept of posting, I don't think it is possible to avoid performances.

BeReal tried to fight against inauthentic social performance, but when "BeReal time" arrived at an uninspiring moment, people had the option to either share a bland, meaningless photograph or, more favorably, wait to take their BeReal for a performance they felt happier sharing. On the other hand, when BeReal caught you at a good moment, and you got authentically perform something that made you happy, that joy inspired you to keep using the app. I'd argue it was fundamental to BeReals growth. Importanly though, both cases the "fake" BeReal and the coincidentally "good" BeReal are in different ways misleading.

While misleading performances may be bad, performance itself isn't. Instead it is the context of the performance, that hurts social media. It is the demarcation of the performer and the witness that breeds an unhealthy dynamic.

The witness is a passive role, or as Cheung describes, "reduced to two thumbs and a pair of eyes". It is worth lamenting the agency the user has given up. For the unimaginable amount of time we spend as users, it is hard to articulate what more we do than merely witness. Social media platforms have caged our role of the user as only worthy of contributing feedback. To consume and judge. Reflecting on the vastly mismatched amounts of authentic performance, within seas of judgement and consumption, it is no wonder that these spaces are so dysfunctional.

For a social media of the future to ameliorate this misfit, I think we must re-envision the role of the user. To destroy the demarcation between stage and audience. To empower all users to engage exclusively by creating and contributing, and emphasize collaboration over individual performances. To raise everyone onto a single collaborative stage.

A Reinvisioned Stage

Posts and Content

The kinds of content on this platform will be very open, though the interface will favor long form content that took effort to create. It will feature many standard kinds of social media content, such as

  • Captioned photographs
  • Videos
  • Statements (think tweets)
Except it will also support other kinds of files worthy of archiving, such as
  • PDFs
  • Sheet music
  • 3D files
  • Datasets
  • Source code
  • Custom interactive HTML interfaces
  • Thumbnail references to mediums outside the walled garden of the platform, like books, paintings, films and TV shows, websites
The other kinds of "posts" can be interactive and structured. A user could post a
  • Manually curated feed
  • Folder of posts
  • Remixing of someone else's post
  • Living/open document
  • Structured and formalized debate
  • Comment section
  • Question (could have special restricitions on how people respond)

Semantically, there wouldn't be a distinction between a picture and a comment section, both would be posts of their own.

Links and Hypermedia

The platform would allow anyone to create a link to anybody else's post. These links would be visually equivalent to a user's own post, but following it would instantly take you to someone else's page. There are no opaque links either, a link is a thumbnail of it's contents, sort of like Wikipedia's link hover function â€” you always know where it is taking you.

The result of this is that a collection of posts will be an assemblage of a person's creations and inspirations. Links are the fundamental sharing mechanism that connects the platform.

Pages and Spaces

Profiles can create spaces, where posts will be displayed, sort of like a Desktop on a computer. To ease the burden of navigation, users will have the ability to create feeds (which are posts themselves), but users still retain a great deal of control over the structure and appearance of their space.

Spaces are not just owned by one person. They are often shared, and we could consider a variety of leadership structures for shared spaces, such as mods (Reddit/Discord) maybe with something like a GitHub pull request, to allow submissions. An important premise is to have everything be small and composable enough so that users can design combinations of posts into larger structures that fit their creative visions.

The Extension Section

The most signficant construct in this app is the extension section, which is intended to supercede the notorious comment section. Extensions are posts people make as a response to someone else's post. It could be as simple as a comment, but also could be a post as a response or continuation of the original author's work. Like might be found in an academic journal. An extension could be a link to a post on someone else's page, or a link to a post on your own page. Extensions are about making connections, they are the glue of the platform.
