Introduction, Inspiration and Design Philosophy

In Notes on the Synthesis of Form, architect Christopher Alexander suggests that we must design forms that fit their context. According to Alexander, good fit is extremely hard to invent or describe, but that by identifying misfits between the form and context, we can create natural designs. This proposal intends to systematically resolve misfits between today's cultural context and forms of social media, resulting in a distributed media platform whose form enriches it's context.

Another crucial resource was Joanne K. Cheung's reflection User's vs Muses, which paints the 21st century as the "Century of the User", and questions the dynamic that users play when we engage with mainstream technologies today. Her reflections on how designers can empower users, were highly influential.

Finally, this paper draws on the vision of Doug Engelbart — whose mission was to augment human intelligence. For Doug in 1968, it was the desire to augment human intelligence that lead him to invent the user interfaces and many computing features we would still consider advanced today. In this paper we update Doug's questioning to ask, can technology be used to augment a culture?.

When reading the rest of this paper, it is important to remember that we often think of a social media as successful when it is
  • Visited many times per day
  • Fun and digestibile enough that we use it for hours on end
  • Socially and politically powerful
  • Embedded in and altering our social fabric
  • Making its parent company wealthy beyond comprehension
I'd suggest though, that these qualities actually identify an un-successful social media. In many respects this project seeks to intentionally oppose these values, so I ask that you try to resist skeptecism based on these metrics.

Dynamics of Engagement

When we call an application design as a "social media", we could be referring to any number of forms. However, the primary thread between them being is always idea that the "users" create the content. Theoretically, this means that the creators and the consumers are the same people, but for the most popular social media platforms that this is hardly true. The most popular social medias operate more as an entertainment platform than a virtual community space. And they provide two primary roles of interacting with the platform, performers and witnesses.

The performer is the person creating something to be seen and appreciated by an audience. Platforms vary greatly in the kinds of performances they encourage, long performances like Youtube, short performances like TikTok, social performance like Instagram, even a clever quips to Twitter is a form of performance. To be concrete, the act of creating for an audience, distinguishes performance from normal use. Sending a snapchat of your face is not a performance, though a snap story is — commenting well wishes on someones post is not a performance, but commenting a joke for the other viewers is.

A performance can leave its consumers with an impression of joy, entertainment, bitterness, rage or jealousy. Though performance is inherent to creating on social media, as I mentioned earlier — it's the very thing that is social media. Therefore, it is crucial that we are intentional about the kinds of performances we encourage and obstruct when designing the form of our platform. Neil Postman's critical analysis of the television in Amusing Ourselves to Death, guides us that "there is a determinate relationship between form and content". However, a wide variety of performance mediums exist throughout popular social medias, though many of the issues remain. I propose that the aspect of form that must be revised is the dynamics of engagement as a user. Uplifting users to be more than witnesses. Instead it is the context of the performance, that hurts social media. It is the demarcation of the performer and the witness that breeds an unhealthy dynamic.

The witness is a passive role, or as Cheung describes, "reduced to two thumbs and a pair of eyes". For the unimaginable amount of time we spend as users, it is hard to articulate what more we do than merely witness. Social media platforms have caged our role of the user as only worthy of contributing feedback. To consume and judge. It is worth lamenting the agency we user give up. Reflecting on the vastly mismatched amounts of authentic performance, within seas of judgement and consumption, it is no wonder that these spaces are so dysfunctional. But when our capabities as users range from either scrolling, liking or commenting — what more is there to do?

BeReal is an interesting example of forcing all users to be participants. They uplift the user to take part in the content, but still prevent anyone from making a meaningful contribution. BeReal encourages performaning in a medium that is "real", effortless and frequent, a better app would embrace the power of performance and encourage something meaningful and intentional.

Before diving deeper, I want to reflect on the emotional dynamic we want users to experience. We want them to feel curious, inspired and capable of performing their own creations. We want people reaching out learn from each other. We want people to feel empowered. To me, this is vastly different than how I feel browsing other social media, amused, passive, and semi-conscious.

For a social media of the future to ameliorate this misfit, I think we must re-envision the role of the user. To destroy the demarcation between stage and audience. To empower all users to engage exclusively by creating and contributing, and emphasize collaboration over individual performances. To raise everyone onto a single collaborative stage.

A Reinvisioned Stage

Posts and Content

The kinds of content on this platform will be very open, though the interface will favor long form content that took effort to create. It will feature many standard kinds of social media content, such as

  • Captioned photographs
  • Videos
  • Statements (think tweets)
Except it will also support other kinds of files worthy of archiving, such as
  • PDFs
  • Sheet music
  • 3D files
  • Datasets
  • Source code
  • Custom interactive HTML interfaces
  • Thumbnail references to mediums outside the walled garden of the platform, like books, paintings, films and TV shows, websites
The other kinds of "posts" can be interactive and structured compositions of other posts. A user could post a
  • Manually curated feed
  • Folder of posts
  • Remixing of someone else's post
  • Living/open document
  • Structured and formalized debate
  • Comment section
  • Question (could have special restricitions on how people respond)

Semantically, there wouldn't be a distinction between a picture and a comment section, both would be posts of their own.

Links and Hypermedia

The platform would allow anyone to create a link to anybody else's post. These links would be visually equivalent to a user's own post, but following it would instantly take you to someone else's page. There are no opaque links either, a link is a thumbnail of it's contents, sort of like Wikipedia's link hover function — you always know where it is taking you.

The result of this is that a collection of posts will be an assemblage of a person's creations and inspirations. Links are the fundamental sharing mechanism that connects the platform.

Pages and Spaces

Profiles can create spaces, where posts will be displayed, sort of like a Desktop on a computer. To ease the burden of navigation, users will have the ability to create feeds (which are posts themselves), but users still retain a great deal of control over the structure and appearance of their space.

Spaces are not just owned by one person. They are often shared, and we could consider a variety of leadership structures for shared spaces, such as mods (Reddit/Discord) maybe with something like a GitHub pull request, to allow submissions. An important premise is to have everything be small and composable enough so that users can design combinations of posts into larger structures that fit their creative visions.

The Extension Section

The most signficant construct in this app is the extension section, which is intended to supercede the notorious comment section. Extensions are posts people make as a response to someone else's post. It could be as simple as a comment, but also could be a post as a response or continuation of the original author's work. Like might be found in an academic journal. An extension could be a link to a post on someone else's page, or a link to a post on your own page. Extensions are about making connections, they are the glue of the platform.

Means of Discovery

Before we begin with designing the process of discovering new content, we must ask why we want users to see more content? Most social media applications have a unified reasoning — to drive engagement and subsequent revenue. In our platform the reasons for discovery should mimick those of any culture; to listen, learn and enjoy.

While you could argue that other social media platforms are also places for exploring these virtues, the existing medium of discovery fundamentally limits how users are capable of exercising them. The primary medium being each of our own personalized recommendation systems. These algorithms that are mathematically based in the convergence and boxing, lead to misleading echo chambers and rabbit holes. Fundamentally they are often based on engagement metrics, which rewards shocking, misleading, extremist and divisive content.

Finally they cripple us with an infinite stream of information — regardless of it's nature – our brains are not equipped to handle such scales of information, especially when the recommendation has been engineered to satisfy us. We are left with a Huxleyan craving for more that incapacitates our ability to meaningfully participate with social media in any way other than consume. Witnessing this much content doesn't lead to enrichment, it leads to addiction. And in a market driven funded by attention it is clear that these companies will do anything to keep us engaged.

For techno-optimists who might look at current AI models and say "why can't we instead just build algorithms to encourage healthy cultural discourse", I ask even if it were possible to develop an algorithm that exclusively recommended content that truly enriches those who listen, learn, and enjoy it — would an infinite amount of this stuff even be good? Is that the best way to increase the capability of the users?

Instead I ask that we leverage community as a recommendation system. Let's use Instagram as a case study. To discover new content we can reliably visit the For You page with it's mystical recommendation sytems, or often we can see what our friends are reposting and sharing with us. Instagram stories is obviously not perfect, but the content we see in it often reflects a relatively diverse set of minds, not just our own. Moreover, when we do get information or misinformation through Instagram stories, it is accompanied by a critical piece of context — who posted it. Knowing why we are seeing helps ground the content.

In the proposed social media, there will be no "the algorithm", but there will be a community acting as a meta-algorithm recommending, filtering and sharing each others performances. Although it may fall under some similar issues regarding the proliferation of attention-grabbing content, there is fundamental difference; someone shared it with a reason and purpose under their own inspiration. Another significant advantage, is that if all the content is manually curated and shared, there is no infinite anything. And when the scale of a collection overwhelms its the curator, then it will probably overwhelm its receiver.

Another other means of discovery will be search, except with one twist. The twist is inspired by an experience I had doing archival research at the UC Berkeley rare records room. I was writing an essay about the 1969 Native American occupation of Alcatraz, and I went to the Berkeley library to get my hands on some original photographs. I sat down at a table and traded with the librarian, my information about the photos for a pair gloves. When she returned she placed a large record box onto the desk. Inside it wasn't just my photos, but also personal letters, official records, and other resources that were invaluable to my project. Importantly, I never would have known to search for these records, it was thanks to the curationists manual work that I was able to access this information. Search on this platform will function similarly — returning manually curated collections rather than a single match.

Ranking and Ratings

If this platform were to have any success, eventually it would have to grapple with the issues brought on by scale. How could a user locate a valuable extension in a sea of ten thousand, we need some way of sorting.

Many places use likes or some other metric of mass appeal. Mass appeal favors familiarity and recency bias, often drowning other good ideas. To understand the cost of mass appeal, let's look at the fall of local news media in favor of national news stations. Mass appeal is why the main sources for news tell us nothing relevant about our lives, rather entertaining us with distant, marginally applicable information, while important local changes go unnoticed. Mass appeal serves only to cyclically make famous thoughts and thinkers more well-known. It sounds somewhat wrong but democracy may not suit internet.

On Youtube on the other hand, the comment promotion algorithm certainly uses likes, but takes into account other details as well. Based purely on speculation, I have noticed that positive comments are often ranked higher than angry or negative comments. This neglects the nuances regarding the context why this feeling is being had, possibly subjugating forms of dissent. Leveraging AI tools we could theoretically filter for content based on an emotional response, but it is hard to choose emotions to prioritize without excluding other valuable ways of repsonding.

Instead, we focus on demotion. And I believe there is one kind of response we want explicitly to demote — shallowness. This will conversely highlight extensions and performances that are considerate and thoughtful. To do this a combination of metrics would be used — one of which being to look at follow ups. If people extend a performance with curiosity and consideration, then we can assume the original performance was likely valuable. Similarly we will look for reflection as a response. While it may seem weird to prioritize these two actions that don't really feel natural to social media, once a status quo is understood that extending other's work with curiosity and reflection, it will feel natural. It is the design decisions beneath this algorithm that will set the status quo.

The final thing that the ranking algorithm will promote is connections. Interesting and unique connections will be greatly rewarded — simultaneously fighting convergence and echo chambers, while adding a fundamental medium of discovery that exceeds the power of search and immediate community.

Why is any of this good?

This project seeks to implement and extend Martha Nussbaum's ethical framework of morality as capabilities. Although, through the internet today, anyone is technincally capable of significantly more than ever before, the malicious practices of large technology corporations significantly influence what people are actually capable of. Tristan Harris shares, our thoughts and actions are not as independent as we may think. By demanding attention and reducing users to mindless consumers, these companies don't necessarily control what we are capable of, but rather what we do.

The power of this application isn't in the features it has, but in the culture it creates. By combining a culture of creating, with wide user capabilities, this application inspires people to actually exercise these capabilites. Moreover it encourages people to do together, meaning that capabilties are shared between people.
